Distribution of marks

Proportion of students from each of the schools

It can be seen that Gabriela Pereira school has a greater proportion of students, so this property is not distinctive

Age Distribution

The greatest part of the dataset are students of age 15-18 which are distributed almost uniformly. Pupils who are 19 years or older represent only a small portion, so the dataset is focused mainly on high school students.

Sex distribution

The proportion of women is a bit bigger but we can say that the proportion is balanced.

Comparing the best and the worst

We took a look at four groups - Students with more than 17 points in the last test, All students, Students that got 0 points and since that could be affected by the high number of students that got 0 points we also considered Worst students(4 to 6 points) where those who got 0 are excluded. In this graph, we compared the mean of their marks (G1, G2 and G3). You can simply choose a category and a barplot that compares means in those groups appears. Some results we concluded are:

Mother's education

The mean in the best group considering mother's education was by 0.7 higher than overall mean. However the worst group was not that far from the overall mean when excluded the "0-points" students.

Single is good

Majority of the best students are not in the relationship (0.055) compared to mean for all studetns. Interestingly enough, the value is quite high among the students that received 0 points in the last test.

Oh-oh, that's what makes you succesful!

Probably the most natural question that comes to our minds when talking about school results is whether there are some indicators that impact students' marks whether negatively or positively and if so, how significant the impact is. We took a look at the following three:

Parents' education impact

Impact of parent's education on academical results

By following the most significant points(the biggest ones) we can see that average raises noticably.

Family size impact

Impact on family size on academical results

The dots are coloured by the fraction (number of students with that amount of points from one of the groups divided by the size of the groups). Seeing that the colors are distributed the same way on both left and right we might say that the size of the family doesn't bring a crucial impact on the grades.

Girls vs Boys

Comparison of results based on gender

Male students seem to have slightly better results than female students

Correlation matrix

Choosing appropriate categories for the regression

To get the highest precision in our model we wanted to choose categories that correlate enough, with a desired coefficient and under the assumption that the correlation is linear. Plotting a correlation matrix gave us some hints that we used later when creating the model.

Grades do make an impact

Which is a quite obvious observation but also turned out to be helpful

Failures

have rather negative correlation which does impact the grades in an expected way

Some irrelevant (but still interesting) correlations

Smart marriage

There is a correlation that implies man with higher education also tend to marry women at similar knowledge level

Alcoholics stay alcoholics

Students with higher alcohol consumption during weekdays also have a tendency to drink during the weekend. Also students that like to go out are more prone to drinking alcohol.

Linear model

We know that G3 is discrete variable ranging from 0 to 20, so the linear regression might not be the best way of predicting results, but in the following we will use logistic regression as well. We are also right to think that the observation are independent of each other.
